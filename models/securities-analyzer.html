+ First principles & § 2(a)(1): open the reference

The statutory definition: § 2(a)(1), Securities Act of 1933

"Any note, stock, treasury stock, security future, security-based swap, bond, debenture, evidence of indebtedness, certificate of interest or participation in any profit-sharing agreement … transferable share, investment contract … certificate of deposit for a security … any put, call, straddle, option, or privilege on any security … or, in general, any interest or instrument commonly known as a 'security' …"

Intentionally broad, drafted to reach the countless schemes devised to use other people's money on the promise of profits. An instrument either is a security or it is not; there is no in-between.

Three operating principles

  • The "duck" test. If it functions like a security (people invest money expecting profits from the efforts of others), it is one, whatever the label says.
  • The regime is mandatory. Once an instrument is a security, federal law attaches automatically; you cannot re-label your way out.
  • No waiver, no opt-in (§ 14). Any clause waiving compliance is void, and parties cannot opt into securities law if the instrument does not qualify.

How this tool is organized

First, classify the instrument. Then apply the governing test (Landreth for stock, Reves for notes, Howey for everything else), check the exceptions, and reach a result. Not every path uses every stage.

Educational analytical aid, not legal advice. Models the leading federal framework (Securities Act of 1933 § 2(a)(1); SEC v. Howey; Reves v. Ernst & Young; and progeny).
